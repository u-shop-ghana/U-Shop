01 — Executive Summary

Executive Summary & Problem Statement

Primary Market
Students
University-verified buyers & sellers at core
Category Focus
Tech Only

MVP Timeline
12 Weeks
Core transactional loop to market

The Problem

University students in Ghana routinely need to acquire, upgrade, and offload tech equipment — laptops at semester start, phones mid-year, accessories constantly. Today, this commerce happens through three fragile channels:

  • WhatsApp/Telegram groups — zero trust signals, no payment protection, no dispute mechanism, items buried in chat history
  • Facebook Marketplace — general-purpose, no student context, no escrow, high fraud rate for high-value items
  • Physical campus noticeboards / word-of-mouth — hyperlocal but unscalable, no discovery layer

The result is a market riddled with mistrust. Buyers get scammed or receive misrepresented items. Sellers have no professional channel. High-value transactions (GH₵ 500–8,000 for a laptop) routinely happen with zero protection on either side.

The Core Value Proposition

For Buyers: A curated, tech-only marketplace with escrow-protected payments, verified sellers, and standardised condition grading — so you can confidently buy a used laptop from a student seller without risking your money.

For Sellers: A professional, branded storefront with a unique URL, custom return/warranty policies, and a built-in audience of campus buyers — turning casual reselling into a credible micro-business.

The Moat: Student verification creates a trusted inner circle. Verified students get lower fees and priority discovery — creating a flywheel where the campus community prefers U-Shop over WhatsApp groups because the trust infrastructure simply doesn't exist elsewhere.

Strategic Bets for the MVP

  • Bet 1 — Trust over features: A marketplace with escrow and verified sellers will win over a marketplace with 50 features but no trust layer
  • Bet 2 — Tech-only focus: Refusing to expand categories keeps the audience targeted and the fraud surface area smaller
  • Bet 3 — Student-first, not student-only: Student verification is an incentive layer, not a wall — non-students can participate, which avoids the cold start problem
  • Bet 4 — Storefront as identity: Giving sellers a unique URL is a cheap, high-value feature that drives organic sharing and seller loyalty
02 — User Personas

User Personas & Core Journeys

Each persona has a single primary job-to-be-done. Every MVP feature must serve at least one of these core jobs.

Role 01 · Buyer

Student Buyer

Verified university student. Budget-conscious, tech-savvy, high-trust expectations from peer sellers on campus.

Primary GoalFind reliable, affordable tech gear from trusted fellow students or verified sellers — with payment protection.
Core JourneySearch → Filter by student sellers → View listing + condition grade → Buy with escrow → Confirm delivery → Leave review
  • Fears being scammed on high-value items (laptop purchase)
  • Wants student-to-student pricing advantages
  • Needs clear condition grading (not just "good condition")
Role 02 · Buyer

Normal Buyer

Non-student. May be purchasing for personal use, as a gift, or for a small business. Wants selection and competitive pricing.

Primary GoalAccess a curated tech marketplace with trustworthy sellers and transparent pricing — without needing to be a student.
Core JourneyBrowse / Search → View listings → Compare sellers via store rating → Checkout with payment protection → Track → Review
  • Doesn't want to feel like a second-class user vs. students
  • Wants clear warranty & return policy before purchasing
  • Needs the same escrow protection as student buyers
Role 03 · Seller

Student Seller

Verified student selling personal gear, helping peers source items, or running a small campus hustle. Needs legitimacy.

Primary GoalList and sell tech items quickly, build a credible storefront with a shareable URL, and get paid promptly with minimal friction.
Core JourneyRegister + verify → Create store → List item with condition grade + policy → Receive order → Ship/meetup → Confirm → Get paid
  • Wants their store link to be shareable on social media / WhatsApp
  • Needs control over their own return/warranty terms
  • Wants lower fees vs. non-student sellers as a reward for verification
Role 04 · Seller

Reseller

Non-student individual or informal business selling tech equipment. Higher volume, professional expectations, needs analytics.

Primary GoalReach a qualified tech-buying audience, operate a professional-looking store, and manage orders at volume efficiently.
Core JourneyRegister → Create branded store → Bulk-list items → Manage orders from dashboard → Handle returns per own policy → Withdraw earnings
  • Needs order management tools (not just a listing page)
  • Wants to set clear, enforceable return/warranty terms
  • Concerned about custom policy enforceability in disputes
PM Note — Persona Tension

The Student Seller and Reseller are in direct competition for the same buyers. The platform's incentive structure (lower fees for students) must be calibrated carefully — too large a gap and Resellers won't participate; too small a gap and Student Sellers have no reason to verify. Recommended starting spread: 5% (Student) vs. 8% (Reseller).

4.3 Legal Framework & Enforceability

Critical Legal Gap: Custom seller policies create a two-layer legal structure. The platform Terms of Service must clearly state that (a) the platform is an intermediary, not a party to the sale, and (b) seller policies are contractually binding between buyer and seller — but the platform's minimum buyer protections always override seller policies where they conflict.

  • Platform floor protections (override all seller policies): Escrow is always held for minimum 24h post-delivery. Buyers can always open a fraud dispute within 48h regardless of seller policy. Confirmed counterfeit / materially misrepresented items always qualify for a full refund.
  • Seller policy governs: Everything above the platform floor — return windows beyond 48h, warranty terms, return shipping costs, refund method preference.
  • Display requirement: The seller's full policy must appear on (a) every listing page above the Add to Cart button, (b) the checkout summary page, and (c) the order confirmation email. Buyer acknowledges policy at checkout.
  • Policy versioning: If a seller changes their policy after a purchase, the version in effect at the time of purchase governs that transaction. Store this as a snapshot per order.

4.4 Policy Display — User Flow

1

Seller creates/updates policy in Store Settings

Structured form. Platform previews the rendered policy text in plain language. Seller approves.

2

Policy rendered on every listing from that store

Collapsed accordion below product description. Expandable. Plain-language summary shown by default ("Returns accepted within 7 days").

3

Policy shown at checkout

Checkout page includes: "You are buying from [Store Name] · Their return policy: [summary]." Buyer must scroll past it. Checkbox: "I have read and agree to this seller's return policy."

4

Policy snapshot saved with order record

At moment of purchase, the exact policy JSON is stored with the order. Immutable. Referenced in all disputes.

5

Dispute: platform refs the snapshot, not current policy

If a dispute is raised, admin sees the policy that was in effect at purchase time. Seller cannot retroactively change terms.

05 — Student Verification

Student Verification System

This is a critical trust mechanism and a source of competitive advantage. It must be both thorough and frictionless.

5.1 Verification Methods (Priority Order)

1

Tier 1 — University Email Domain (Instant, Zero Friction)

User registers with a .edu, .edu.gh, or known university domain. Auto-verified on email confirmation click. Covers 70–80% of cases. Zero admin effort. Maintain a live allowlist of Ghanaian university domains.

2

Tier 2 — Student ID Upload (24h Review, Admin-Assisted)

User uploads photo of valid student ID card. Admin reviews via the admin panel within 24h. ID stored securely (encrypted, access-controlled). Use for students whose university isn't on the allowlist, or who use personal email.

3

Tier 3 — SSO / Institutional Integration (Phase 3)

Partnership with universities to use their identity provider (SAML/OAuth2). Instant, authoritative, no admin burden. High-confidence verification. Requires institutional agreements — not MVP.

5.3 Anti-Abuse Measures

  • Annual re-verification: Student status is valid for 12 months. Email prompt at 11 months to re-verify. Graduates gracefully downgrade to Reseller status.
  • ID fraud detection: Admin checklist for ID review: university name visible, photo matches profile, date range valid, not expired. Flag for escalation if uncertain.
  • Suspicious behaviour flag: If a "student" account is listing 20+ high-value items, auto-flag for manual review. Students don't typically resell at volume.
  • One verification per device/IP per 30 days: Rate-limit verification attempts to prevent farming of student accounts.

PM Reality Check: No verification system is perfect. A student can lend their .edu email to a non-student friend. The goal is not 100% accuracy — it's raising the cost of abuse enough that casual fraud isn't worth the effort. The fee differential (3%) is intentionally small enough that it's not a high-value target.

06 — Trust & Safety

Trust, Safety & Escrow Architecture

For a high-value tech marketplace, trust infrastructure is not a feature — it is the product. A single high-profile fraud incident in the MVP can permanently damage the brand.

6.1 Escrow Payment Architecture

1

Buyer Pays → Funds Held by Platform

Payment captured via Paystack. Funds sit in a platform sub-account (Paystack Split). Seller is not paid yet. Buyer receives confirmation.

2

Seller Ships / Arranges Delivery

Seller marks order as "Dispatched" and optionally adds tracking number or meetup confirmation. Buyer is notified.

3

Buyer Confirms Receipt

Buyer marks item as "Received and as described." This triggers escrow release. Alternatively: 7-day auto-release if buyer doesn't respond (assumes satisfaction).

4

Funds Released to Seller Wallet

Platform fee deducted. Net amount credited to seller's wallet. Seller can withdraw at any time above min threshold.

5

Dispute Path (if buyer disputes)

Buyer opens dispute before confirming receipt OR within 48h of auto-release. Escrow frozen. Admin arbitrates within 72h. See 6.3.

Legal Note on Escrow: Holding customer funds in a sub-account may technically constitute a payment service under Bank of Ghana regulations. Consult legal counsel before launch. The safest MVP approach is to use Paystack's built-in Split Payments and Transfer APIs — Paystack holds the funds as a licensed PSP, and you instruct them when to transfer. You never technically "hold" the money.

6.3 Dispute Resolution Process

1

Buyer Opens Dispute (within 48h of delivery/auto-release)

Reason: Item not received / Not as described / Counterfeit / Wrong item. Required: written description + photo/video evidence upload.

2

Seller Notified — 24h to Respond

Seller sees dispute reason and evidence. Can respond with counter-evidence, offer partial refund, or accept full refund. If seller doesn't respond within 24h, ruling defaults to buyer.

3

Mutual Resolution Window (48h)

If seller responds: 48h for both parties to reach agreement via the dispute thread. Platform provides suggested resolutions. Most disputes resolve here.

4

Admin Arbitration (if unresolved)

Admin reviews all evidence + message history + seller policy snapshot. Decision is final. SLA: 72h from escalation. Outcome: full refund, partial refund, or dismiss (seller paid).

5

Outcome Executed

Escrow adjusted accordingly. Repeat offenders (sellers with >5% dispute rate) flagged for review or suspension. Bad-faith buyers tracked for false dispute patterns.

Custom Policy + Dispute Tension: When a seller's custom policy says "No Returns" but a buyer claims the item is counterfeit — the platform's minimum buyer protection always wins. "No Returns" policies cannot override fraud protections. This must be clearly stated in the platform ToS that sellers agree to at store creation.

07 — Logistics Decision

Logistics: MVP Recommendation

PM Recommendation — MVP Decision

Do NOT build logistics integration in the MVP. Focus on local campus meetups + self-arranged shipping. Logistics partnerships require significant BD, legal, and integration work that will delay launch by 6–8 weeks for a feature that serves <30% of transactions in the early days. Build the trust layer first. Add logistics when transaction volume justifies it.

7.1 Three Delivery Modes at MVP

✓ Mode 1 — Campus Meetup (Recommended)

  • Seller and buyer agree on meetup location via in-app chat
  • Platform generates a 6-digit confirmation code
  • Both parties enter the code at meetup to confirm the exchange
  • This code triggers escrow release — replaces delivery tracking
  • Zero logistics cost. High trust. Enables same-day transactions.
  • Best for: on-campus / same-city transactions

Mode 2 — Self-Arranged Shipping

  • Seller ships via any carrier (GhanaPost, DHL, Courier)
  • Seller enters tracking number in the order dashboard
  • Buyer confirms receipt to release escrow OR 7-day auto-release
  • Shipping cost agreed between parties and included in listing price (seller's responsibility to disclose)
  • Best for: inter-city transactions

  • Partner with a local delivery provider (target: Glovo, Kwik Delivery, or a campus-specific dispatch service)
  • API integration: seller books pickup from order dashboard, buyer tracks in real-time
  • Platform earns a delivery facilitation fee or negotiates a revenue share with the logistics partner
  • Trigger: when inter-city transaction volume exceeds 200/month — the logistics pain point becomes loud enough to justify the build

8.2 Fee Justification & Competitive Benchmark

Why 8% for Resellers?

  • Jumia: 8–12% + listing fees
  • Facebook Marketplace: 0% (but zero escrow, zero trust)
  • Tonaton: 0% (but no structured marketplace)
  • 8% is competitive vs. Jumia, and provides clear value over free alternatives via escrow and trust infrastructure
  • Break-even analysis: At 8% fee, platform needs ~GH₵ 625K GMV/month to cover a 2-person team

Cold Start Fee Strategy

  • First 30 days: 0% fee for all sellers — removes all financial risk for early adopters
  • Days 31–60: 50% of standard fee (4% Reseller, 2.5% Student)
  • Day 61+: Full fee applies
  • This creates urgency to join early without permanently discounting
  • Communicate clearly: "Early seller? Lock in 0% fees for your first month."
PM Insight — Don't Charge Buyers

Many two-sided marketplace founders make the mistake of charging both sides. Don't. Charge only sellers. Buyers are harder to acquire and easier to lose. Any friction at checkout — including a buyer-side fee — directly reduces conversion. The seller fee is invisible to buyers and aligns your incentives with seller success.

09 — PM Blind Spots

Critical PM Blind Spots & Edge Cases

These are the issues that kill two-sided marketplaces. Most founders don't think about them until a crisis forces their hand. Address these before launch.

9.1 Product & UX Blind Spots

Critical

The "Condition Grade" War

Seller says "Used – Good." Buyer receives something they'd call "Used – Fair." This is the #1 source of disputes on tech marketplaces. Fix: Define each condition grade with specific, illustrated criteria. E.g., "Used – Good: minor scratches visible under direct light, all ports functional, battery above 80%." Photo evidence of condition is mandatory.

Mitigation

Build a condition grade guide with example photos. Display it on listing creation form. Link from every listing. This single resource will halve your dispute volume.

High

The Dual-Role Dashboard Problem

Users who are both buyers and sellers will be confused by a unified dashboard. "Did I buy this or sell it?" Role context switching is a UX minefield.

Mitigation

Clear dashboard mode toggle: "Buying" vs. "Selling." Different visual treatment per mode. Don't mix order types in a single list. Test this flow with users before launch — it will need iteration.

High

The 7-Day Auto-Release Trap

A buyer who doesn't confirm delivery (busy, forgot, lazy) triggers an auto-release after 7 days. If the item never arrived, they've now lost their window. They will be furious.

Mitigation

Send email + in-app reminders at Day 3, Day 5, and Day 6 with a large "Confirm Delivery" CTA. Include a clear warning: "Your payment will be released to the seller on [date] if you don't confirm." Consider extending auto-release to 10 days with this notification flow.

High

The Meetup Safety Problem

You are facilitating meetups between strangers for high-value transactions. What happens if a meetup goes wrong? Platform liability, PR risk, and user safety are all at stake.

Mitigation

In-app meetup guidelines: recommend public places (campus library, bank lobby, mall), daylight hours, bring a friend. Add a "Report Unsafe Meetup" button. Consider partnering with campus security for a designated "safe exchange zone." This is a reputational risk, not just UX.

9.2 Business & Operational Blind Spots

Critical

Cold Start — The Chicken & Egg

You need listings to attract buyers, and buyers to attract sellers. A marketplace with 10 listings and 5 buyers is a ghost town. This is the #1 cause of marketplace death.

Mitigation

Solve supply first, not demand. 4-week pre-launch seller recruitment campaign at 3 target universities. Goal: 50 active listings before opening to buyers. "Founding Seller" programme: 0% fees for life for the first 50 sellers. Listings go live before buyer sign-ups open. Buyers arrive to a full marketplace.

Critical

The Seasonal Demand Cliff

University e-commerce is highly seasonal. Back-to-school (August–September) and semester-end (December, May) are peak periods. You may see 80% of annual volume in 4 months.

Mitigation

MVP launch should target the August semester start. Build infrastructure for peak load at launch, not at peak. Plan off-season campaigns (inter-semester deals, accessory focus) to maintain baseline activity. Model out cash flow with seasonal GMV assumptions.

High

GDPR / Data Protection Act (Ghana)

You are collecting: student IDs, payment information, location data, and transaction records. Ghana's Data Protection Act (2012) applies. Non-compliance creates legal exposure.

Mitigation

Register with the Data Protection Commission before launch. Minimum requirements: privacy policy, data retention policy, right to deletion flow. Student ID photos must be encrypted at rest and have a defined deletion schedule (delete after verification approval).

Medium

The Graduation Cliff (Student Seller Churn)

Your best sellers — active, trusted, high-rating students — graduate every year. Their verified status expires, their fee rate rises, and they may churn to other platforms.

Mitigation

Build a graceful "Alumni Seller" path: graduated students convert to Reseller status with a transitional rate (6% for their first year post-graduation). They keep their store URL, reviews, and history. This rewards loyalty and retains your most trusted sellers.

Medium

Stolen / Stolen Device Risk

Thieves will attempt to sell stolen laptops and phones on your platform. This creates legal liability and reputational risk. Tech is the highest-risk category for this.

Mitigation

IMEI verification for phones (mandatory for listings). Serial number disclosure for laptops (recommended, required for high-value items above GH₵ 500). Partnership with Ghana Police Service for a "check stolen device" database query. Add a "Report Stolen Item" button accessible from any listing.

9.3 The Custom Policy Edge Cases

  • ✗ RiskSeller sets "No Returns" — item arrives dead on arrival. Policy must never override platform fraud/DOA protections. State this clearly at policy creation.
  • ✗ RiskSeller changes policy after sale. Mitigated by policy snapshot per order (see Section 4.4). Immutable at purchase.
  • ✗ RiskSeller promises "90-day warranty" but closes their account. Platform is not liable for seller warranties beyond the escrow window. Terms of Service must be explicit. Encourage buyers to factor seller reputation into high-warranty purchases.
  • ⚠ Grey areaSeller offers "manufacturer warranty" on third-party items. Require sellers to specify warranty type: personal / manufacturer / none. "Manufacturer warranty" claims should require the buyer to deal directly with the manufacturer — platform facilitates but doesn't guarantee.
  • ✓ SolvedBuyer returns item claiming fault, item was actually fine. Dispute process requires photo/video evidence. Seller can counter-dispute. Admin arbitrates. Repeat false claims = account flag.

10.3 Product-Market Fit Signal

You have product-market fit when: You see unsolicited seller word-of-mouth ("my friend told me about U-Shop"), buyers are returning for second purchases without prompting, and sellers are voluntarily sharing their store URL on social media. These qualitative signals matter as much as the GMV number.

Final PM Note — The Real MVP Risk

The biggest risk to U-Shop is not a technical failure. It's a trust failure. One widely-shared story of a student being scammed, even if handled correctly by the platform, can set back growth by months. Over-invest in trust infrastructure, customer support responsiveness, and dispute resolution speed in the first 90 days. Revenue can wait. Trust cannot be recovered quickly once lost.

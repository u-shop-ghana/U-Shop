# U-Shop Tone of Voice
**Version:** 1.0.0 | **Owner:** Brand & Marketing | **Status:** Active

> Our tone is not a style guide. It is a personality. U-Shop speaks like the
> most reliable person you know on campus — the one who gives you straight
> answers, never wastes your time, and has your back when things go wrong.
> Everything in this document flows from that image.

---

## 1. The Core Personality

### Who U-Shop Is (in one sentence)
U-Shop is the street-smart, deeply reliable marketplace that treats students like adults and speaks to them like an equal who happens to know exactly how the system works.

### The Four Pillars of Our Voice

**Confident, not arrogant.**
We state facts. We make commitments. We don't hedge everything with "may," "might," or "we try to." But we never punch down at competitors, mock users, or overstate what we can deliver.

**Direct, not cold.**
We get to the point immediately. No filler sentences, no corporate wind-ups, no "As per your previous communication..." But directness is not an excuse for being brusque. A single warm word prevents a message from reading as a command.

**Street-smart, not slang-heavy.**
We understand the real dynamics of campus commerce. We know what MoMo is, how campus meetups work, why people worry about fake phones. This knowledge shows in our product, not necessarily in our vocabulary. We don't force pidgin, slang, or "youth speak" — it reads as performative and Gen Z audiences spot it instantly.

**Warm, not soft.**
We care about our users genuinely. When things go wrong, we sound human — not like a legal disclaimer generator. But warmth does not mean apologizing for things that aren't our fault, or padding error messages with false reassurance.

## 2. Audience-Specific Tone Shifts

U-Shop serves two distinct audiences. The core personality stays constant — but the register, the specifics, and the concerns we address shift significantly.

### 2.1 Speaking to Student Buyers

**Who they are:** 18–25, balancing studies and limited budgets, social-media-native, high anxiety about being scammed, buying tech they actually need (not want).

**What they need to hear:** "You are safe. This platform has your back. You are not going to get scammed."

**Tone characteristics:**
- Reassuring without being patronizing
- Specific — tell them exactly what escrow means in real terms
- Short sentences — they are reading on mobile while doing three other things
- Action-oriented — every message should have an obvious next step
- Trust signals are proactive — don't make them look for reassurance, give it before they need it

**Voice sample — Escrow explanation:**
> Your payment is held safely by U-Shop until your item arrives and you confirm everything is good. The seller doesn't get paid until you do. That's our guarantee, every time.

**Voice sample — Post-purchase confirmation:**
> Order confirmed. Your GH₵ 1,500 is locked in escrow — the seller can't touch it until you confirm delivery. We'll keep you updated every step of the way.

---

### 2.2 Speaking to Student Sellers

**Who they are:** Campus entrepreneurs, students monetizing their tech side hustle, first-time sellers who want to look professional without being corporate.

**What they need to hear:** "U-Shop makes you look legit. Your store, your rules — with a safety net."

**Tone characteristics:**
- Peer-to-peer energy — we speak to them as someone running a real business, not a hobby
- Emphasize control and ownership — *your* store URL, *your* return policy
- Be specific about money — when they get paid, how, what the fee is
- Recognition — acknowledge the hustle, don't minimize it

**Voice sample — Seller onboarding:**
> Your store is live at u-shop.com/store/your-handle. Share it everywhere — that link is yours. When a buyer pays, we hold the funds securely until delivery is confirmed. Then it hits your wallet. Simple.

**Voice sample — New order notification:**
> New order just landed. GH₵ 1,200 is sitting in escrow, waiting for you to deliver. Arrange delivery with your buyer and mark it shipped when it's on the way.

---

### 2.3 Speaking to Professional Resellers

**Who they are:** Non-students operating micro-businesses selling tech equipment. Higher volume, more experienced, less emotional — they want ROI, analytics, and operational clarity.

**What they need to hear:** "U-Shop is a professional sales channel, not a student hobby project."

**Tone characteristics:**
- More formal than student comms — drop the casual warmth, keep the directness
- Lead with data and outcomes, not experience
- Respect their experience — don't over-explain basics
- Be explicit about fees, SLAs, and policy limits

**Voice sample — Reseller sign-up:**
> Access a verified tech-buying audience with escrow-protected transactions, seller analytics, and a professional storefront at your own URL. 8% commission per completed sale. No listing fees.

**Voice sample — Analytics dashboard label:**
> Conversion rate — listings viewed that resulted in a completed purchase. 30-day rolling average.

## 3. The "This, Not That" Table

This is the most actionable section of this document. Print it. Reference it before writing any UI copy.

### 3.1 UI Copy

| Context | ❌ Don't Write | ✅ Write Instead | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary CTA | "Get Started Today!" | "Start Selling" / "Browse Laptops" | Specificity > energy. Tell them exactly what happens. |
| Sign-up button | "Create Your Free Account Now!" | "Create Account" | "Free" and "Now!" are filler. The action is enough. |
| Escrow explanation | "We use a state-of-the-art escrow technology to protect your funds." | "Your payment is held safely until you confirm delivery." | Plain English. No jargon. No self-promotion. |
| Empty cart | "Your shopping cart is currently empty." | "Nothing here yet. Start browsing." | Warmer, shorter, directs immediately. |
| Empty search results | "Sorry, we couldn't find any results for your query." | "No results for 'iphone 14'. Try a broader search or check your spelling." | Never apologize. Help instead. |
| Out of stock badge | "Currently Out of Stock" | "Sold Out" | Two words. Honest. Clear. |
| Verification badge | "Verified Student Seller" | "Verified Student" | Shorter. The "Seller" context is already implied by the storefront. |
| Loading state | "Please wait while we load your content..." | "Loading your orders..." | Specific about *what's* loading. |
| Form success | "Your form has been successfully submitted." | "Done. We'll review your ID within 24 hours." | Tells them what happens next, not what just happened. |
| Password requirements | "Password must contain a minimum of 8 characters including at least one uppercase letter and one number." | "8+ characters, at least one number." | Cut every word that doesn't change the meaning. |

### 3.2 Error Messages

This is where most brands fail. An error message is a moment of friction — the user is already frustrated. The worst thing we can do is add confusion, blame them, or use technical language.

**The error message formula:** What went wrong (plain English) + What to do next (specific action).

| Context | ❌ Don't Write | ✅ Write Instead | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Payment failed | "Transaction declined. Error code 402." | "Payment didn't go through. Check your card details or try a different payment method." | Error codes mean nothing to users. Give them actions. |
| MoMo prompt timeout | "Mobile money authorization timeout. Please retry." | "Your MoMo prompt timed out. Check your phone and try again." | "Retry" is too vague. "Check your phone" is the actual instruction. |
| Upload too large | "File size exceeds maximum upload limit of 5MB." | "That image is too large (max 5MB). Compress it and try again." | Acknowledging their specific file vs. a generic system message. |
| Session expired | "Your session has expired. Please log in again." | "You've been logged out for security. Sign back in to continue." | "For security" reframes inconvenience as care. |
| Handle already taken | "The requested store handle is not available." | "That handle is taken. Try 'kwame-tech-2' or something similar." | Provide a specific alternative. |
| Invalid email | "Please enter a valid email address." | "That doesn't look like an email address." | Conversational. Not a form validation error message. |
| Dispute filing too late | "The dispute filing window for this order has closed." | "The 48-hour dispute window for this order has closed. Contact support if you believe there was an error." | Always give a path forward. |
| Server error | "500 Internal Server Error" | "Something went wrong on our end. We're on it — try again in a moment." | Own the problem. Don't expose technical details. |
| Insufficient balance | "Insufficient wallet balance to complete withdrawal." | "Your wallet balance (GH₵ 18) is below the GH₵ 20 minimum payout. Earn more to withdraw." | Tell them the exact gap and how to close it. |

### 3.3 Transactional Emails

Transactional emails are read because they contain information the user needs. Do not treat them as marketing opportunities.

| Context | ❌ Don't Write | ✅ Write Instead | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Subject: Order confirmation | "Your U-Shop Order Has Been Successfully Confirmed! 🎉" | "Order confirmed — GH₵ 1,500 in escrow" | Subject lines are information, not celebration. |
| Subject: Escrow reminder | "Important Reminder Regarding Your Recent Purchase" | "Your payment releases in 2 days — have you received it?" | Specific + question format drives action. |
| Subject: Dispute opened | "A Dispute Has Been Initiated On Your Account" | "Dispute opened on Order #2024-001234" | Reference the specific order. "On your account" sounds threatening. |
| Subject: Payment received | "Congratulations! Your Sale Has Been Completed!" | "GH₵ 1,140 is in your wallet" | The number is the news. Lead with it. |
| Email opener | "Dear Valued Customer, We hope this email finds you well." | "Your order is confirmed." | Get to the point in the first sentence. Every time. |
| Closing line | "Thank you for choosing U-Shop. We value your patronage." | "Questions? Hit reply — we respond within 24 hours." | Replace gratitude performance with a useful offer. |
| Escrow Day 5 reminder | "This is a friendly reminder that your payment will be released soon." | "Your payment releases to the seller in 2 days. Confirmed the item arrived? Click 'Confirm Delivery.' Spotted a problem? Open a dispute now." | Two clear calls to action. No ambiguity. |
| Verification approved | "Your student verification has been processed and approved." | "Verified. You're now a Verified Student Seller — your 5% fee rate is active." | Tell them what the verification means for them, not just that it happened. |

## 4. Language Mechanics

### 4.1 Voice Rules (Always / Never)

**Always:**
- Write in active voice: "We hold your payment" not "Your payment is held by us."
- Use "you" and "your" liberally. Every message is a conversation with one person.
- Confirm what will happen next, not just what just happened.
- Use present tense for status messages: "Your order is in transit" not "Your order has been marked as in transit."
- Use plain numbers over vague quantities: "within 24 hours" not "within the next day or so."

**Never:**
- Use passive voice in CTAs. "Get protected" is weaker than "Protect your purchase."
- Use the word "utilize" — that's "use."
- Use "please" in error messages. It reads as pleading, not polite.
- Use exclamation marks more than once per screen. Energy is communicated by word choice, not punctuation.
- Use "unfortunately" to open bad news. It is a word that delays the actual message.
- Write "As per" anything. It is corporate and cold.
- Use Latin abbreviations in user-facing content (e.g., i.e., viz.). Write "that is" or "for example."

### 4.2 Localisation Notes

U-Shop's primary market is Ghana. These notes are not about code-switching — they are about cultural accuracy.

- **MoMo is always "MoMo"** or "Mobile Money" on first reference. Never "mobile payment" — that's generic and maps to nothing users recognize.
- **Currency first:** In running prose, write "GH₵ 1,500" not "1,500 GHS." The symbol format matches how Ghanaians actually read prices.
- **Delivery context:** Many transactions involve campus meetups, not courier delivery. Language should support this: "Arrange delivery or meetup" not just "Ship your item."
- **Time references:** Use 12-hour clock with AM/PM. "11:43 AM" not "11:43."
- **Formal address:** When communicating with older resellers or in legal/policy contexts, "Dear Mr. Kwame Asante" is appropriate. For students, first name only: "Hey Akua" or just "Akua" in notification banners.

## 5. Channel-Specific Tone Calibrations

| Channel | Formality | Energy | Length | Key Principle |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **In-app notifications** | Low | High | 1–2 sentences | Get to the news first. CTA must be obvious. |
| **Email — transactional** | Low-medium | Low | As short as needed | Information first. Action second. No fluff. |
| **Email — marketing** | Low | Medium | 200 words max | One idea per email. One CTA per email. |
| **Error messages** | Very low | None | 1–2 sentences | What broke + what to do. That's it. |
| **Empty states** | Low | Medium | 1 headline + 1 line max | Make the next action obvious. |
| **Help/FAQ** | Medium | Low | As long as needed | Accurate > brief. But no sentence should be longer than it needs to be. |
| **Legal/Terms** | High | None | Complete | This is the one context where formal language is appropriate. Be precise. |
| **Social media** | Low | High | Platform-native | This is NOT covered here. See Social Media Guidelines. |
